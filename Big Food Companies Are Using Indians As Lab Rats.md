### **The Shift: From Satisfaction to Addiction**
For the longest time, food companies cared about your tongue, about making something that tasted good and hit the spot. But at some point, that changed. They stopped trying to satisfy you and they started trying to hack you.

And to understand when that shift began, we need to go back to 1969. This is where Howard Moskowitz comes in. Moskowitz was a Harvard-trained mathematician hired by the US Army to solve a strange problem. Soldiers were throwing away their ready-to-eat meals half-eaten no matter what they served. The military needed to know why.

What Moskowitz discovered changed everything. It's called **sensory-specific satiety**. When you eat flavors that are too bold and distinct, your brain gets overwhelmed and tells you to stop. It's an evolutionary mechanism meant to prevent overeating. But Moskowitz thought, what if you could bypass that mechanism? What if you could make flavors interesting enough to keep eating, but never distinct enough to trigger the stop signal? You could essentially make people eat forever.

So for the next three decades, Moskowitz optimized everything — Prego spaghetti sauce, Dr. Peppers, soups, pickles, salad dressings, everything. And his experiments led him to discovering what's called **the bliss point**: a food tastes good because of a precise mathematical balance of salt, sugar, and fat.

In Michael Moss' book *Salt, Sugar, Fat*, he talks about how he found this out the hard way. If you ever made salt-free cornflakes and took a bite, all you taste is metal. And that's because salt masks the off notes, the metallic taste that comes from processing. Without it, you taste what this food actually is: an industrial byproduct. So to make it palatable — or hyper-palatable to be precise — you need astronomical amounts of salt, sugar, and fat. Change the amount of any one of them, you have to adjust the others.

Suddenly, a new profession was invented: **food engineering**.
